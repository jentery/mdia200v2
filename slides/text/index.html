Now for a lecture about media + text . . .

four reasons we might study media

1. Media are everywhere.

2. Media are fundamental to how people relate.

3. Media are history machines.

4. Media are also speculation machines.

Artistic medium / Arts media, with an emphasis
on making, performing, and expressing.

Communications media, with an emphasis on
storage (data), transmission (signals),
exchange (information), and reporting
(journalism and the media)

Definition: Media are material agents
for transforming “this” into “that.”

Definition: Technologies are means
of reproduction and communication.

Definition: Technologies are means
of reproduction and communication.

Example: Let's consider text as a medium.

My operating system produces an ASCII or
Unicode character whenever I press a key.

Text is an agent for transformation.

Text may be intended for multiple destinations.

Text may be stored in several locations.

People notice media the most during moments
like these. Connections are down. Work is lost.
Rhythm and flow are interrupted.

Text is a messenger or carrier.

Text is subject to time and weather.

Text is "remediatied."

People instill their trust in media,
but media exceed our control.

“You can't have media
without resistance in the material.”

Once we grasp media as materials,
we must also learn to approach them critically and
responsibly as forms of mediation in the world.

First Theme
Media are congealed labour and techniques.

Text is labour because it is
a process and not just a thing.

People do not merely “use” media.
They design, compose, revise, and circulate
work with and through them.

Asking whether a given work is immersive is less
productive than asking why and when it wants
media like text to recede from attention.

Text may instruct you.

“Please use other door.” “Stop.” “Click here.”

Text may instruct a computer.

Text may get meta.

Recall the final page of Tomine's “Translated”:
“I wonder how old you are now as you read this.”

Text may become a spectacle.

A pop-up in your browser reads,
“WARNING! YOUR COMPUTER MAY BE INFECTED!” Or YHCHI's THE M00D 0F THE M0MENT fills your screen and asks . . .

The labour of media dissolves into the very files
and records intended to preserve it.

No media capture the totality of a process and
its motivations: how and why something was made.

Second Theme
Media are social practices.

Media connect people while also alienating them
from each other and their work. That's the
complex contradiction of media's durability—of putting your work into circulation.

Repetitive activities are further
standardized through formats.

Publication formats are means of nesting and
presenting text in familiar ways. They include books, articles, magazines, and websites as well as novels, short stories, and comics, for example.

File formats, on the other hand, are means of
storing and circulating text for computer work.
They include TXT, DOC, RTF, HTML, PDF,
and CSV, for instance.

Media are vehicles for memory and habits.
They are formatted and standardized to encourage consistency across groups and situations, and
communities of media practice gather around them.

Third Theme
Media are experiences.

The effects of media are not homogeneous. Media
do not determine how they are used or experienced even if they discipline everyday activity
and facilitate habituation.

Media foster an embodied, personal experience, and
they capitalize on that experience.

The time and resources required to study and
enjoy media—to play and imagine without
always being productive—is necessary for
everyone, not just an elite few.

Fourth Theme
Media are culture.

Williams: the “residual,” “dominant,”
and “emergent” elements of culture

Text as a residual element of culture:
This is text as a past institution that's still
somehow active in the present.

Text as a dominant element of culture:
This is text as a norm that subsumes the past
while also presenting itself as . . . the present.

Text as an emergent element of culture:
This is text as a novel technique, practice,
perception, or value, if not a
new occupation or class.
