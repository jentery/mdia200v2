Let's begin with a common claim. Perhaps you've
heard it in popular discourse or the media.

"Violent video games cause
violent behaviours."

Let's define what "violent" could mean here and
compare it with terms such as
"aggressive" and "competitive."

An important question emerges from the
use of "cause violent behaviours" as well:

Do people tend to passively, actively, or selectively engage media? How about video games in particular?

The first one is a perennial question in Media Studies.

Media Effects Research

In effects research, media are forms
that impact culture and society.

"Violent video games cause violent behaviours," for instance. The content and particulars of a game
don't really matter for this claim. It speaks generally or vaguely of them as a form.

The effect is usually one of three types:
direct, minimal, or third-person.

The "direct effect" position would claim
that violent games cause audiences
to think or behave violently.

Academic research does not, however,
demonstrate a link between game violence
and violent crimes in North America.

See, for instance, the American Psychological Association's "Resolution on Video Games":
"Attributing violence to violent video gaming is not scientifically sound and draws attention
away from other factors." Note: my literature review is limited to North America.

The "minimal effect" position would claim
that violent games encourage or reinforce
aggressive behaviours.

Violent video games may temporarily alter
people's moods, for instance.

See "The Effect of Online Violent Video Games on Levels of Aggression"
by Hollingdale and Greitemeyer as well as "Short-Term Effects of Competitive Video Games
on Aggression: An Event-Related Potential Study" by Sun, Hao, and Liu.

However, meta-analytic studies demonstrate
practically no long-term effects of violent
video games on aggression.

See "Do Longitudinal Studies Support Long-term Relationships between Aggressive Game Play
and Youth Aggressive Behaviour? A Meta-analytic Examination" by Drummond et al.

The "third-person effect" position would
claim that, "Violent games affect
other people but not me."

You might say (for good reason) that violent
video games are repugnant, for instance.

This is a moral position.

Matt McCormick asks, "[I]s participating in simulated violence even where there is no victim itself
somehow morally objectionable? Is there anything wrong with going through the motions of an immoral act,
and if so what is it?" ("Is It Wrong to Play Violent Video Games?")

Let's recap before we proceed:
direct, minimal, and third-person effects.

Now for a few theories related to
the media effects paradigm . . .

Cultivation theories of media effects would
claim that violent video games lead
players to accept games as reality.

You might say (for good reason) that violent
video games desensitize players to video game
violence, which players tacitly accept as the norm.

Yet desensitization to video game violence does not
correlate with desensitization to violent crimes.

See James Shanahan and Michael Morgan for more on cultivation theories. Also see Gao et al.,
"Long-Time Exposure to Violent Video Games Does Not Show Desensitization on Empathy for Pain" and
Lengersdorff et al., "Neuroimaging and Behavioral Evidence that Violent Video Games Exert No Negative
Effect on Human Empathy for Pain and Emotional Reactivity to Violence."

Uses and gratification theories (UGT) of
media effects would claim that violent video
games fulfill people's wants and needs.

You might say (for good reason) that violent
games appeal to power fantasies or escapism.

However, this position relies heavily on
individual choice and doesn't tell us why and
when people want to escape or fantasize about power.

See Harold Lasswell for more on uses and gratification theories. Also see Sherry et al.,
"Video Game Uses and Gratifications as Predictors of Use and Game Preference," and Bonny and Castaneda,
who write: "UGT focuses on sustained player engagement with videogames as a function of motivation to seek
gratification from videogames" ("To Triumph or to Socialize? The Role of Gaming
Motivations in Multiplayer Online Battle Arena Gameplay Preferences."

Social learning theories of media effects would
claim that violent video games model
behaviours that players mimic.

You might say (for good reason) that violent
games encourage fans to produce media
imitating violent behaviours.

However, this position risks assuming that players are
passive vessels for reproducing violence.

See Albert Bandura for more on social learning or "social cognitive" theories. Shao and Wang write:
"According to Bandura's social cognitive theory, violent video games can initiate adolescents' observational learning.
In this situation, not only can they imitate the aggressive behavior of the model but also their understanding
and acceptability about aggression may change" ("The Relation of Violent Video Games to
Adolescent Aggression: An Examination of Moderated Mediation Effect").

Agenda-setting theories of media effects would
claim that politicians and the media influence what people think about violent video games.

You might say (for good reason) that moral panic
narratives and funding initiatives shape
what people hear and learn about video games.

This corresponds with the "Goldilocks effect":
each generation believes its experience of media
was the correct or best one.

See "Teaching Us to Fear" by Markey and Ferguson for more on
moral panics and scapegoat narratives involving video games.

Some other issues to consider:

Violent crime rates in the US tend to drop
when AAA games are released.

Gamer cultures invested in violent video
games are often toxic and hostile.

Video games don't cause these cultural phenomena,
but what approaches (beyond media effects)
help us to better understand them?

See Markey et al., "Violent Video Games and Real-World Violence: Rhetoric Versus Data,"
and Kishonna Gray's Intersectional Tech: Black Users in Digital Gaming.
